The Wolf of Wall Street (2013)

Genre: Biography, Comedy, Crime | Runtime: 180 min | Director: Martin Scorsese

Based on the true story of Jordan Belfort, from his rise to a wealthy stockbroker living the high life to his fall involving crime, corruption and the federal government. In the late 1980s, Jordan Belfort (Leonardo DiCaprio) lands a job as a Wall Street stockbroker for L.F. Rothschild. After the firm collapses in the Black Monday crash, Belfort finds work at a boiler room brokerage selling penny stocks. Realizing the immense commissions, he starts his own firm, Stratton Oakmont, with his partner Donnie Azoff (Jonah Hill). Their company quickly grows into a powerhouse, with Belfort and his colleagues living a life of extreme wealth, drugs, and debauchery—until the SEC and FBI begin closing in.

FrameDeer Thoughts

Martin Scorsese delivers a wild, outrageous, and brilliantly excessive satire of American greed that stands as one of his most energetic and controversial films. The Wolf of Wall Street is a three-hour rollercoaster of debauchery that somehow manages to be both a condemnation of and celebration of the excesses it portrays.

The film's genius lies in its ability to make the audience complicit in Belfort's lifestyle. We're simultaneously repulsed by and attracted to the world of limitless money, drugs, and hedonism. Scorsese never moralizes or judges—he simply presents the insanity with such infectious energy that we understand why people would be drawn to this life, even as we recognize its emptiness.

Leonardo DiCaprio gives a career-defining performance as Jordan Belfort, delivering what may be his most physically committed and emotionally unhinged role. From his motivational speeches to his drug-fueled breakdowns, DiCaprio commands the screen with a ferocious energy that never flags throughout the epic runtime.

Jonah Hill is equally brilliant as Donnie Azoff, creating one of cinema's most memorable sidekicks. Their chemistry is electric, and Hill matches DiCaprio's intensity beat for beat. Margot Robbie announces herself as a major star with her portrayal of Naomi Lapaglia, Belfort's second wife who becomes both an accomplice and victim of his lifestyle.

The film's breakneck pacing and stylistic flourishes—including fourth-wall breaking narration and surreal visual gags—create a tone that's both hyper-realistic and cartoonish. This approach perfectly captures the absurdity of Belfort's world, where reality becomes increasingly distorted by wealth and power.

Final verdict: A masterful, provocative film that's as entertaining as it is disturbing. Scorsese's most purely fun film in decades, but one that leaves you questioning your own moral compass by the end.
